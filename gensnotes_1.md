## 00 | Preface — Who Is This Person?

There is a certain type of person who keeps showing up in the same places across different eras of internet culture — someone who writes too much, thinks too loudly, and refuses to settle into a single category. That is, in essence, what this document is about.

My name is Gentaro. I was born on February 17, 1997, in Japan. I am a bilingual writer, engineer by training, customer support professional by current occupation, and creative universe-builder by compulsion. I operate online under the persona **Mina Eureka** — full name Wilhelmina Eureka Ernst — a 29-year-old fictional intellectual headquartered in Gigapolis, an imagined mega-city that serves as the backdrop for my philosophical and speculative writing. The line between Gentaro and Mina is intentionally blurry. One is the man living in Cyberjaya, Malaysia, answering customer support tickets for Booking.com. The other is the voice that has written over 1,800 articles, composed 260 songs, and built a multimedia creative empire across a dozen platforms. Both are real. Both are me.

This document is an index of that life — organized by era, domain, incident, and output — written for anyone who wants to understand not just what I've done, but how those things connect.

## 01 | Origins — Early Life in Japan (1997–2014)

I was born into an ordinary Japanese household and grew up through an era that now feels like science fiction in retrospect. The 1990s internet was just beginning to bleed into everyday life, and the Japan I was raised in still had one foot firmly in an analog, community-oriented culture and another stepping cautiously into screens.

**2000–2004: The Outdoor Years (50% outdoor)**
The earliest years were characterized by a kind of physical, unself-conscious childhood that feels increasingly rare. Playing outside, community rhythms, the absence of smartphones — this era left a residue of sensory groundedness that I still return to in my writing when I try to anchor abstract ideas in bodily experience.

**2005–2009: Peak Outdoor Era (90% outdoor)**
If there is a golden age of unmediated physical experience in my personal history, it is this period. Sports, outdoor play, local community — a 90% outdoor existence that formed the baseline of what I think human beings are supposed to feel like before the interfaces take over.

**2010–2014: The Great Indoor Turn (50% outdoor)**
The shift happened gradually, then all at once. Anime, YouTube, manga — the cultural products of the early streaming era colonized the attention I had previously given to the physical world. Sports remained, but increasingly as a counterweight to screen time rather than the default mode of being. This is also the era of what I privately call the "Three Middle School Incidents": a portrait-drawing controversy, an interpersonal encounter of the awkward adolescent variety, and a fistfight in the tennis club. These events, small as they may seem in retrospect, encode something important about the social negotiations and minor rebellions that define adolescent self-formation.

**The Ligament Rupture — 2015**
Before entering university, I underwent ACL (anterior cruciate ligament) surgery and spent a full year in rehabilitation. This is not merely a medical footnote. Physical incapacity at a formative moment tends to redirect energy inward, toward reading, writing, thinking. I believe this injury was a structural event in my intellectual development — the year of recovery is when the indoor world, the world of ideas and digital culture, stopped being an escape and started being a primary habitat.

## 02 | Academic Formation — Engineering, Twice (2015–2021)

### Yamagata University, Faculty of Engineering (2015–2019)

I entered Yamagata University's engineering program on April 1, 2015 — a date that, in Japan, carries the weight of formal beginnings. Four years in Yamagata, a mid-sized city in the Tohoku region, gave me a rigorous technical foundation and an acute awareness of regional Japan: its quietness, its gradual depopulation, the particular texture of a city that is not quite rural but not urban enough to feel plugged in. I would later write about this experience when I returned to Japan temporarily and observed firsthand how yen weakness and demographic decline were reshaping these places.

Engineering as a discipline trains a specific kind of thinking — systematic, falsifiable, oriented toward function. I absorbed this framework fully and then spent the next decade finding ways to apply it to everything that engineering programs explicitly exclude: philosophy, narrative, culture, meaning.

### Ibaraki University, Faculty of Engineering (2019–2021)

I continued into a graduate program at Ibaraki University, graduating on March 31, 2021. Two institutions, two degrees, both in engineering. The decision to pursue graduate study reflects something consistent about my character: a bias toward depth over exit, toward building credentials that compound, even when the returns are uncertain. The engineering training ended here, but its influence on how I structure arguments, analyze systems, and think about cause-and-effect never did.

## 03 | Career Trajectory — From Engineering to BPO to Customer Strategy (2021–Present)

My professional history is, on its surface, a story of pivot and turbulence. Underneath, it is a deliberate accumulation of customer-facing expertise with an eye toward technical sales leadership. Here is the record:

### ITAPEX Corporation (April 2021 – March 2022)
My first corporate role post-graduation. One year in a Japanese company — long enough to internalize the rhythms and expectations of Japanese corporate culture, and to realize that those rhythms were not where I was building toward.

### Cyber Fortress Corporation (August 1–31, 2022)
One month. This is the entry in my history that most requires explanation, and which I have chosen simply to list without apology. There are professional situations that clarify values through incompatibility rather than fit. This was one of them.

### Teleperformance (September 5 – October 23, 2022)
Apple Customer Support. My first entry into the BPO (Business Process Outsourcing) world, and my first sustained experience in bilingual customer service. The contract concluded as scheduled. This brief assignment planted something important: the recognition that customer support, done with genuine attention and language precision, is a form of high-stakes communication that rewards the kind of analytical care I had developed in engineering.

### Tech Mahindra (April 13 – July 14, 2023)
Amazon Customer Support. Three months. The pattern here — BPO roles, Japanese-language support, major global platforms — was beginning to crystallize as a deliberate niche rather than a series of accidents. Each contract added to a body of experience: handling escalations, translating not just language but expectation and cultural register, operating within the rhythms of global service infrastructure.

### Concentrix — Booking.com Support (January 8, 2024 – Present)
The longest continuous professional engagement in my history. Japanese-language customer support for Booking.com, one of the world's largest travel platforms. This role has given me sustained exposure to the mechanics of customer experience at scale — complaint patterns, resolution frameworks, the emotional labor of de-escalation, the operational reality of global customer service. I view this not as a destination but as the current, longest phase of deliberate capability-building.

**The Strategic Logic:** Customer support for global tech platforms in Japanese is not a random career path. It is a specific position at the intersection of technical product knowledge, language leverage, and customer psychology — three components that are individually common but jointly rare, and that together form the skill base for technical sales, solutions consulting, or customer success leadership in international markets.

## 05 | The Creative Universe — Mina Eureka & Genesis Vault

This is the part of the self-introduction that requires the most space, because it represents the largest sustained investment of time and imagination in my life.

### The Persona

**Mina Eureka** — full name Wilhelmina Eureka Ernst — is a 29-year-old blogger and philosopher based in Gigapolis, a fictional megacity that serves as the setting for a running speculative universe. She has blue long hair, studied AI at Lorenzio International University, and operates under the motto *"来た、見た、勝った"* (Veni, Vidi, Vici). Her three names encode a philosophy: *Mina* (to cherish what is yours), *Eureka* (the discovery, the flash of insight), *Ernst* (seriousness, earnestness, the willingness to follow an idea to its conclusion).

The persona is not a mask. It is an amplification — a version of my intellectual voice freed from the social constraints that real names and real faces impose. When I write as Mina, I write with a directness and authority that serves the content better than the hedging that attaches to personal identity.

### Genesis Vault

Genesis Vault is the media project I have been building for years. As of the most recent count:

- **Note:** 1,200 articles
- **Substack:** 138 articles
- **Patreon:** 1,070 posts
- **Audius:** 260 songs
- **Pixiv:** 850 posts

Combined with the Genesis Vault count of 1,800+ articles, the total creative output across platforms exceeds **3,500 individual works**. This is not a side project. This is a life's work running parallel to the professional track.

**Content domains include:**
- Philosophy (Stoicism, Talmudic thought, ethics of meaning and inequality)
- Geopolitics (Iran, the Sahel region, Southeast Asia dynamics)
- Technology and AI (tool evaluations, AI democratization, automation's effects on labor)
- Finance and economics (Nikkei analysis, crypto market dynamics, Web3 failures)
- Social commentary (BPO industry, Gen Z conservatism, K-pop fan culture, investment fraud)
- Creative fiction (the Gigapolis universe, characters including Alto the quantum economist and Lumina the distributed AI)

**The Mina Eureka Voice:** Accessible, casual Japanese prose that prioritizes genuine insight over academic distance. Not elevated or editorial — conversational, direct, often playful, always substantive. The mission is to make complex ideas feel like discoveries rather than assignments.

### The Gigapolis Universe

Gigapolis is a fictional megacity that serves as the recurring setting for speculative and philosophical content. Its characters — Alto, Lumina, and others — serve as vehicles for exploring ideas that are too abstract to anchor in real-world journalism but too concrete to treat as pure philosophy. The universe grows organically across articles, podcast-style dialogue posts (the Eureka Cast series), and narrative essays.

### Technical Infrastructure

Genesis Vault is not just a content project — it is an experiment in independent media infrastructure:
- Automated blog system built on **Netlify and GitHub**
- **Telegram bot** for post submission
- Experiments with **Make.com and Zapier** for social media automation
- Exploration of multi-platform AI workflows (Claude, Zhipu, Lovable)
- Notion-based content organization

The goal is an algorithmic-independent economic and cultural zone — a creative ecosystem that does not depend on platform favorability or advertising revenue. Membership-based revenue and direct audience relationships are the target model.

The most significant personal loss in the timeline, handled in the self-history with the same laconic notation as a traffic accident. I think this reflects something about how I process: by continuing to move, by converting experience into output, by keeping the record going regardless of what the record contains.

## 10 | Philosophical Orientation — How I Think

**Core influences:**
- *Meditations* by Marcus Aurelius — the book I return to most. The Stoic insistence on the gap between event and interpretation, on the universe as change, on the self as the only jurisdiction you genuinely control.
- Talmudic thought — not as religious practice but as an intellectual methodology: the tradition of argumentation, of taking an idea to its logical limit, of preserving minority opinions alongside majority conclusions.
- Web3 and crypto — not as ideology but as a laboratory for thinking about decentralization, value, trust, and the structural vulnerabilities of systems designed without central authority.

**Operating principles:**
- The universe changes constantly; meaning is not found but constructed.
- Life is subjective, which is not a limitation but a design feature.
- Algorithms are not neutral infrastructure but political entities — building outside them is both a creative and an ethical choice.
- Seriousness and playfulness are not opposites. Mina Eureka holds both simultaneously, and that tension is the source of the most interesting writing.
